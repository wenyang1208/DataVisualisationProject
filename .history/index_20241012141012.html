Average Unemployment Rate in Malaysia by State (2023)

In 2023, there were 169,800 jobless people in Sabah.

Based on the chart, there is significant variation in unemployment rates across the country. Sabah has the highest unemployment rate at 7.525%, followed by Labuan at 6.75%, indicating potential economic challenges in East Malaysia. Terengganu (4.425%) and Kelantan (3.975%) also report relatively high rates compared to other states.

On the lower end, Putrajaya has the lowest unemployment rate at just 0.825%, followed by Pulau Pinang (2.125%) and Perlis (2.175%). States like Johor, Kedah, and Selangor show moderate unemployment rates between 2.4% and 2.45%.

Overall, those unemployed are generally young people, both graduates and non-graduates, who have had trouble entering the job market. Because of this, the Malaysian government is encouraging companies to open up employment opportunities specifically to young adults.

Time-related Underemployment in Malaysia by Age Group (2023)

In recent years, tertiary-educated workers risk underemployment
without market flexibility, due to the increasing demand for
advanced qualifications in the job market.

In this context, underemployment is defined as working fewer than 30 hours per week while being willing to work more.

In 2023, underemployment in Malaysia shows notable differences across age groups. The 25-34 age group experiences the highest rate, with approximately 72,275 individuals underemployed, equating to 12 icons, each representing 6,000 people. The 45+ age group follows with around 47,775 individuals, or 8 icons. Both the 15-24 and 35-44 age groups have lower underemployment rates, with 32,650 and 30,150 individuals respectively, represented by 5 icons each.

Overall, it highlights that while the 25-34 age group faces the most significant underemployment, underemployment remains a concern across all age groups, particularly for those aged 45 and older.

Larger states like Selangor and Johor tend to have higher GDP growth, while smaller states such as Labuan and Perlis experience lower growth. Exceptions include Sabah, with a large population but low GDP growth, and Putrajaya, with a small population but high GDP growth.

Regions with larger populations, like Selangor, Johor, and Kuala Lumpur, have higher labor force participation rates, while smaller states like Labuan and Perlis show lower rates. Higher GDP growth states, like Pahang and Selangor, have moderate unemployment, whereas lower GDP growth states, such as Sabah and Sarawak, face higher unemployment.
